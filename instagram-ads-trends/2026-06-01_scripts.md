### Script 1: One winner isn't a strategy

**Topic:** Why copying your one winning ad stalls the account — and why creative pillars scale instead.
**Pillar:** Structural Tear-Down
**Hook Type:** Symptom to Root Cause
**Length:** ~105–115s (matches source)

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DTI_VewD2Lk/ — @sampiliero (~111s)
**Source structure breakdown:** Then-vs-now contrast on how Meta serves ads (audience-first → concept-first under Andromeda), an ad-library teardown pulling out three creative pillars (templated product splash, product-in-use, raw UGC), and a close arguing tiny tweaks are dead but pillar concepts + versions compound reach and ROAS.

**On-screen hook text:** ONE WINNER ISN'T A STRATEGY
**Spoken hook:** "Your ad account stalls because every creative is chasing the same buyer."
**Hold line:** "You found one winner, copied it ten ways, and growth flatlined."

**Full script:**
"Your ad account stalls because every creative is chasing the same buyer. You found one winner, copied it ten ways, and growth flatlined.

Facebook ads used to be simple. You picked an audience, you stuffed a bunch of creatives into one ad set, and Facebook served them to that audience. That's gone. Now the creative itself is the targeting. The algorithm decides who sees what based on the ad, not the audience you picked. That's the whole Andromeda shift — you've heard the name by now.

So the game isn't 'find the one winning ad' anymore. It's run a bunch of different concepts that each pull a different slice of the market.

Look at how any billion-dollar brand actually runs their ads. You'll see three or four completely different styles. One is a clean product shot on a templated background — just the product, front and center. Another is the product out in the real world, big bold headline, real context. A third is raw customer video that looks like someone filmed it on their phone. Three concepts. Three totally different feels.

And that's on purpose. The person who converts on the clean product shot would've scrolled right past the raw customer video. The person who buys off the customer video never would've stopped on the polished one. Each concept reaches buyers the others can't touch.

So when people tell you iterations are dead, they're half right. Changing a button color and calling it a new ad — that's dead. But taking a concept that works and building ten real versions of it? That's the whole job. You find the pillars that hit, and you mine them.

Stack concept on concept. That's how you lift your return and your scale at the same time. Copies of one ad just flatten out."

**Why this hook should perform:** Names a symptom every advertiser has lived — scale stops even though the ad "works" — then delivers the full mental model (audience-first → concept-first → pillars → incremental audiences) so the viewer leaves with the actual fix, not a teaser. The depth is what earns the save.

### Script 2: Your landing page is burning spend

**Topic:** The page behind your ad, not the ad itself, is what's spiking cost per lead.
**Pillar:** Hard Truth
**Hook Type:** Expensive Mistake
**Length:** ~160–175s (matches source)

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DMwKogUv4dJ/ — @growzutrue (~174s)
**Source structure breakdown:** A "$10K vs $1M landing page" teardown — several weak pages (no headline, no above-the-fold form, not mobile-optimized, just a hero image) tied to wasted CPC and ~$250 CPL, then the strong page (matching headline, interactive form up top, real pricing, testimonials, mobile-clean) dropping CPL to ~$30.

**On-screen hook text:** YOUR LANDING PAGE IS BURNING SPEND
**Spoken hook:** "You're paying Google for clicks and sending them to a page that can't convert."
**Hold line:** "No headline, no form near the top — just a pretty image they scroll past."

**Full script:**
"You're paying Google for clicks and sending them to a page that can't convert. No headline, no form near the top — just a pretty image they scroll past.

Let me walk you through what a bad one actually looks like, because most of them look fine at first glance. Nice photos. Clean design. Shows off the business. But there's no headline at the top telling me I'm in the right place, and there's no form. You've got about four seconds to grab someone before they're gone. A pretty picture doesn't do that.

And the kicker — half these pages were built by AI, so they're not even optimized for mobile. Wasted space everywhere. The top of the page is just an image. Someone clicks your Google ad — and you paid good money for that click — they land, and it's a photo. Now they've got to scroll to find anything useful. Most of them don't. They hit the back button, and you just paid for nothing.

I see the same mistake over and over. A real estate page with a giant photo of a house at the top. Great. But you have to scroll just to find out where it even is. No form. No pricing. You scroll all the way down before you get to floor plans, and there's no images with them. Their cost per lead is probably sitting around two hundred and fifty bucks, and they have no idea why.

Saw another one — no buttons, no headline, no form at all. Just a page sitting there. What is that even supposed to do with a paid click?

Now compare that to a page that actually prints money. The headline matches the ad word for word — if the ad said move-in ready homes in your area, the page says move-in ready homes in your area. There's an interactive form right at the top that walks them through what they want, one thumb on a phone. Real pricing — actual numbers, not 'contact us.' Real photos. Real testimonials from verified customers. And it loads clean on mobile.

That's the difference between a thirty-dollar lead and a two-hundred-fifty-dollar lead. Same ad. Same traffic. Same spend. The only thing that changed is the page.

So before you touch your bids, before you blame your keywords, go look at the page. The ad isn't your problem. The page eating the click is. I'd fix that first, every time."

**Why this hook should perform:** Reframes a money problem advertisers blame on "the ads" and puts it on the page, then walks three real failure patterns plus the fix — enough specificity that they can audit their own page immediately. The $30-vs-$250 anchor stings and repeats.

### Script 3: Don't touch the winning ad set

**Topic:** Launching new creative into a proven ad set resets its learning and crashes performance.
**Pillar:** Structural Tear-Down
**Hook Type:** Boundary Hook
**Length:** ~65–75s (matches source)

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DYnbHQAM2Dx/ — @sampiliero (~68s)
**Source structure breakdown:** Flat warning that new creatives into existing ad sets crash campaigns, then the rule — every new ad in its own new ad set inside one prospecting CBO, with min/max spend controls so new ads get tested without starving — proven across ~100 brands.

**On-screen hook text:** DON'T TOUCH THE WINNING AD SET
**Spoken hook:** "Stop dropping new creatives into the ad set that's already working."
**Hold line:** "Every time you do, you reset the learning that made it win."

**Full script:**
"Stop dropping new creatives into the ad set that's already working. Every time you do, you reset the learning that made it win, and the whole thing crashes.

So the rule I run across every account is simple: new ads go into a new ad set. Every single time. Never into the one that's already performing.

In one prospecting campaign I'll have ten, twenty, sometimes thirty active ad sets running at once. Sounds insane, I know. But it works because it's a CBO — campaign budget optimization — so Meta gets to push the money toward whatever's actually performing, instead of me sitting there guessing.

And if you're not a fan of CBOs, or you want a tighter grip, you set minimum and maximum spend on each ad set. That forces budget behind your new ads so they get a real test, instead of getting starved out before they ever spend a dollar.

That's the whole system. One core prospecting CBO. New ads into new ad sets. Min and max controls when you want more control.

The winner stays untouched. The new stuff proves itself on its own. That's how you test without torching the thing that's already paying you."

**Why this hook should perform:** A Boundary Hook names a place you shouldn't touch and the cost of touching it. Operators have all watched a winner die after "just one more ad," and the script hands them a clean, repeatable rule instead of a vague warning.

### Script 4: An ad account isn't a funnel

**Topic:** Expensive ads are usually a funnel problem — cold traffic hitting a buy-now ad with no warm-up.
**Pillar:** Future-Proofing
**Hook Type:** Contrarian Reframe
**Length:** ~50–60s (matches source)

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DYpOK7uR1v1/ — @realchasechappell (~55s)
**Source structure breakdown:** A nine-figure brand's three-layer funnel — TOF educational content (myths, swaps, breakdowns), MOF trust stacking (founder content, testimonials, comparisons), BOF retention/LTV (bundles, subscriptions) — amplified across channels. Close: winners build ecosystems, not ad accounts.

**On-screen hook text:** AN AD ACCOUNT ISN'T A FUNNEL
**Spoken hook:** "You don't have an ads problem. You've got a funnel that ends at the first purchase."
**Hold line:** "So you throw cold traffic at a buy-now ad and wonder why it's expensive."

**Full script:**
"You don't have an ads problem. You've got a funnel that ends at the first purchase. So you throw cold traffic straight at a buy-now ad and wonder why it's so expensive.

The brands scaling past nine figures run three layers, and almost nobody copies all three.

Top of the funnel, they teach before they sell. Ingredient breakdowns, myths, swaps, straight-up education. People find them through something useful, not a discount code.

Middle of the funnel, they stack trust. Founder content, customer stories, testimonials, side-by-side comparisons. By the time you land on their website, you already trust them.

Bottom of the funnel, they go after retention. Bundles, subscriptions, repeat-use products that lift the average order and the lifetime value. That's the part that actually pays for aggressive scaling.

Then they amplify all of it at once — Meta, TikTok, email, SMS, affiliates.

That's the shift happening right now. The brands winning aren't building ad accounts. They're building funnels that compound. One ad sells once. A funnel sells for years. So build the layers, not just the account."

**Why this hook should perform:** Flips "my ads are too expensive" into "my funnel is missing layers" and then names all three layers concretely, so it's a reframe with a blueprint attached. Reframes get shared; a reframe you can act on gets saved too.

### Script 5: Stop hiding a good product

**Topic:** Overproduced creative is often a cover for weak product positioning; simple ads win when the product earns it.
**Pillar:** Hard Truth
**Hook Type:** Founder Mirror
**Length:** ~40–50s (matches source)

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DOdxntIjb9l/ — @realchasechappell (~43s)
**Source structure breakdown:** A founder who scaled past $5M by letting the product and lifestyle do the talking — direct product shots, short copy, the story and the making — versus overcomplicated ads. Close: staying authentic and true to the product builds the multi-million-dollar brand.

**On-screen hook text:** STOP HIDING A GOOD PRODUCT
**Spoken hook:** "You're overproducing your ads to cover for a product you won't let speak."
**Hold line:** "You're paying a production tax to hide an offer that should sell itself."

**Full script:**
"You're overproducing your ads to cover for a product you won't let speak. You're paying a production tax — fancy edits, heavy effects, ten messages crammed into fifteen seconds — to hide an offer that should sell itself.

I watched a brand scale past five million a year doing the exact opposite. No complicated ads. Direct product shots. Short, simple copy. Just the product, the story behind it, how it's made. That's it.

And it became a staple — worn by people who actually love it, not because they got paid to post. The product did the talking.

That's the part most people miss. While everyone else overcomplicates the creative, the winners build a machine that converts by staying simple and staying true to what the product actually is.

So before you brief another big production, ask yourself honestly — are you dressing this up because the offer can't stand on its own? If the product's good, simple wins. I'd strip it back before I'd spend more."

**Why this hook should perform:** A Founder Mirror that calls the viewer out — most operators secretly over-edit — then gives them permission (and a real example) to do less. The uncomfortable question at the end is what makes it stick.

### Script 6: Turn off Google's default settings

**Topic:** Google's defaults (Display + Search Partners, loose location/scheduling) and a weak page quietly drain Search budgets.
**Pillar:** Structural Tear-Down
**Hook Type:** Impending Threat
**Length:** ~165–180s (matches source)

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DXWc6NyiWVh/ — @growzutrue (~179s)
**Source structure breakdown:** A full service-business Google Ads setup — pick Leads, drop in page + phone, turn OFF Display Network and Search Partners, tight location radius, separate phrase/broad ad groups, fill all headlines/descriptions, callouts/sitelinks/call extension/promotion, business-hours ad schedule, and a conversion-ready landing page. Optimize after ~60 days.

**On-screen hook text:** TURN OFF GOOGLE'S DEFAULTS
**Spoken hook:** "Google flips on two settings by default that quietly drain your budget."
**Hold line:** "And almost nobody finds them until they've already burned the first few hundred bucks."

**Full script:**
"Google flips on two settings by default that quietly drain your budget. And almost nobody finds them until they've already burned the first few hundred bucks. If you run a service business, here's the whole setup, start to finish.

You go into Google Ads, create a new campaign, and you pick Leads — not sales, not traffic, leads. Drop in your landing page and your phone number, name it, and move on. Don't even touch the bidding yet, just hit next.

Now the first trap. Networks. Google has the Display Network and Search Partners both turned on by default. The Display Network throws your ads onto random apps and websites. Search Partners spreads you across sites that aren't even Google. Both of them are spending your money where your actual buyers aren't. Turn them both off. Every single time.

Next, location. Don't run the whole country if you serve one city. Set a real radius around where you actually work, and keep it tight.

Then your keywords. Build a few ad groups. One group on your tighter, phrase-match keywords. A second group on a different set. And a third group that's broad match only — all broad keywords — kept completely separate, so you can actually see which approach wins. Dump them all together and you'll never know what worked.

For the ad itself — fill all fifteen headlines and all four descriptions. Don't leave them half empty and let Google guess for you. Add images of your real work. Add your callout extensions. Add your sitelinks. Make sure your call extension has your phone number on it, and throw in a promotion if you've got one.

Then the part everyone skips — your ad schedule. Set it to your business hours only. If nobody's answering the phone at three in the morning, stop paying for clicks at three in the morning.

And none of this matters if your landing page can't convert. Form near the top. A real offer. Social proof — your Google reviews, right there where they can see them. Actual photos of your actual work, not stock images. A big call button. And it has to be clean on mobile, with that form still at the top.

Do all that, then give it about sixty days before you start ripping it apart — let it gather data first. Most wasted Google spend isn't your keywords or your bids. It's the defaults you never switched off."

**Why this hook should perform:** An Impending Threat that's already live in their account, then the entire fix delivered step by step — it's a save-and-follow piece because it's genuinely the full tutorial, not a tease. Pulls service-business owners hardest, and it's the run's clearest Google angle.

### Script 7: 96% of your ads will lose

**Topic:** The small share of ads that win share three traits — and none are budget or targeting.
**Pillar:** Structural Tear-Down
**Hook Type:** Specific Blueprint
**Length:** ~150–165s (matches source)

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DYOBtsSJTqN/ — @growzutrue (~163s)
**Source structure breakdown:** Of 1,500 ads tested, ~4% (60) won. The three shared traits — a real person on camera, 30–60s length, a strong first-three-seconds hook (curiosity / contradiction / open-loop, not "check this out") — plus the recurring "ugly ad" skeleton (curiosity opener → hidden problem → specifics + identity → truth shift → CTA).

**On-screen hook text:** 96% OF YOUR ADS WILL LOSE
**Spoken hook:** "Ninety-six percent of the ads you test will lose. The winners share three things."
**Hold line:** "And none of them are budget or targeting."

**Full script:**
"Ninety-six percent of the ads you test will lose. The winners share three things, and none of them are budget or targeting.

Picture testing fifteen hundred ads. Only about four percent come out as real winners. That's sixty ads out of fifteen hundred. So what did those sixty have that the other fourteen hundred and forty didn't?

First, a real person on camera. Almost every single winner had someone talking straight into the lens. Not a polished actor — it should feel like a real customer explaining something, or a founder breaking down a problem, or just a regular person showing the product. And if you're leaning on AI, the second they can tell it's AI, you've lost them. The viewer has to feel like they're getting something useful, because that's what holds attention — and attention is what makes an ad convert.

Second, length. The sweet spot is thirty to sixty seconds. A ten-second ad can grab attention, but it's got no room to name the problem, reveal what's really causing it, show the product, prove it works, and build any trust. You need forty-five, sixty seconds to do all of that. If you are going to run something short, then your landing page or your VSL has to finish the job the ad started.

Third — and this is the big one — the first three seconds. The hook. Most ads open with 'hey, you've got to check out this product.' That's an instant skip. The winners did one of three things. They spiked curiosity — something like, I looked at fifteen hundred ads and the ones that scaled all had the same ugly pattern. Or they ran a contradiction — they challenged something the viewer already believed. Or they opened a loop the viewer had to stick around to close.

And almost every one of those sixty followed the same skeleton. Curiosity opener. Reveal the hidden problem. Break down the fix with real specifics. Make the right person feel seen. Flip their assumption. Then tell them exactly what to do.

Real person, enough room to actually teach, and a hook that earns the next three seconds. Skip those, and you're just funding the ninety-six percent that lose."

**Why this hook should perform:** A Specific Blueprint that trades agitation for proof — the 96/4 stat is concrete, "none are budget or targeting" kills the two excuses up front, and the full three-trait breakdown plus the skeleton makes it immediately usable. Density is the value here.

### Script 8: One ad can't do five jobs

**Topic:** Clean-looking ads lose when each creative tries to carry every message; winners assign one job per creative.
**Pillar:** Hard Truth
**Hook Type:** Symptom to Root Cause
**Length:** ~60–70s (matches source)

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DYmpPirRiPS/ — @realchasechappell (~67s)
**Source structure breakdown:** Several brands each master ONE creative lever — simple problem/solution, demo-driven proof, founder authenticity, unmistakable branding, entertainment, sheer creator volume — with the throughline that each communicates a single idea in three seconds.

**On-screen hook text:** ONE AD CAN'T DO FIVE JOBS
**Spoken hook:** "Your ads look clean and still lose because each one's doing five jobs at once."
**Hold line:** "So the algorithm can't tell who it's for — and neither can the buyer."

**Full script:**
"Your ads look clean and still lose because each one's trying to do five jobs at once. So the algorithm can't tell who it's for, and neither can the buyer.

The brands that actually scale do the opposite. Each creative masters one job, and one job only.

One brand nails simple problem-and-solution. Every ad communicates the same thing in three seconds — better fit, better result, the before and the after. You get it instantly.

Another lives entirely on demos. They show the product proving itself — the stress test, the side-by-side, the thing just working on camera. No claims. Just proof.

Another runs on founder authenticity. While everyone else overproduces, theirs is raw, real, a little educational, kind of personal. That rawness is exactly what builds the trust.

Another wins on pure branding — the packaging, the colors, the shape are so recognizable that even a plain static ad works for them.

And some just win on volume — they flood the feed with customer videos, testimonials, routines, until the sheer amount becomes the strategy.

Notice what none of them are doing — cramming all five into one ad. Pick one job per creative and nail it. Stop building one ad that tries to say everything and gets ignored. Build several that each say one thing perfectly."

**Why this hook should perform:** Symptom ("looks clean, still loses") to root cause ("five jobs at once"), then six concrete archetypes of brands that picked one lever each. It validates that their ads aren't ugly — they're overloaded — and shows the menu of single-job options.

### Script 9: Your ceiling isn't your budget

**Topic:** Hitting a spend ceiling is usually a missing-system problem, not a budget problem.
**Pillar:** Hard Truth
**Hook Type:** Founder Mirror
**Length:** ~55–65s (matches source)

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DNTXhutgmok/ — @realchasechappell (~58s)
**Source structure breakdown:** Speaks to operators who've hit ceilings, arguing most scale without a system — no rule for when to test, scale, or pull back. (Pitch-heavy source; the usable angle is "scaling on feel leaves money on the table." The mentorship pitch is stripped.)

**On-screen hook text:** YOUR CEILING ISN'T YOUR BUDGET
**Spoken hook:** "You hit a ceiling on ad spend and you're calling it a budget problem."
**Hold line:** "It's not the budget. You're scaling on feel, with no rule for when to push."

**Full script:**
"You hit a ceiling on ad spend and you're calling it a budget problem. It's not the budget. You're scaling on feel, with no rule for when to push and no rule for when to back off.

Doesn't matter if you're the brand owner, the media buyer, or running the whole marketing department — you've spent the money, you've had some wins, and now you've hit a wall. I've been there. Almost everyone who scales hits it.

### Script 10: Iterations aren't dead

**Topic:** "Iterations are dead" is misread — tiny tweaks are dead, but real versions of a proven concept are how you scale.
**Pillar:** Future-Proofing
**Hook Type:** Contrarian Reframe
**Length:** ~95–105s

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DTI_VewD2Lk/ — @sampiliero (~111s) *(reuse of the top-ranked source, different angle from Script 1)*
**Source structure breakdown:** Same teardown as Script 1, different beat: the creator separates "tiny iterations are dead" from building real versions of a winning concept — the actual path to incremental audiences and scale. Script 1 takes the pillar-discovery beat; this one takes the iteration-reframe beat.

**On-screen hook text:** ITERATIONS AREN'T DEAD
**Spoken hook:** "Somebody told you iterations are dead, so you killed your best concept too early."
**Hold line:** "Tiny tweaks are dead. Real versions of a proven concept are how you scale."

**Full script:**
"Somebody told you iterations are dead, so you killed your best concept too early. Tiny tweaks are dead. Real versions of a proven concept are how you scale. Those are two completely different things, and mixing them up is costing you money.

Let me draw the line clearly. Changing a button color, swapping one word in the headline, nudging the background a shade — and calling that a new ad. That's the dead kind. It does nothing. The algorithm doesn't care, and neither does the buyer.

Now the other kind. You've got a concept that's already converting — a specific angle, a specific style that's pulling. You take that and you build ten real versions of it. New hooks. New openers. New angles on the same winning idea. Different enough to reach new people, close enough that you keep what's already working.

Think of a winning concept like a vein. When you finally hit one, you don't walk away and go dig a brand-new hole somewhere else. You mine it. You pull everything out of it you can.

But that's exactly what most people do. They find a winner, they get one or two good weeks out of it, they get bored, and they go chasing the next shiny new idea from scratch. Then they wonder why they're always starting over.

Most accounts don't fail from too many iterations. They fail because the owner abandoned a winner way too early. Find the concept that works. Then squeeze every version out of it before you move on to the next one."

**Why this hook should perform:** A Contrarian Reframe against advice the audience has half-absorbed, then a clean distinction (button-swap vs. ten real versions) that resolves the confusion and reframes abandoning winners as the real mistake. Protects their best work, which makes it feel like a gift, not a scold. Sharper bookend to Script 1 — post a few days apart.
