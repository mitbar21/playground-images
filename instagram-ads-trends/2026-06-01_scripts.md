### Script 1: One winner isn't a strategy

**Topic:** Why copying your one winning ad stalls the account — and why creative pillars scale instead.
**Pillar:** Structural Tear-Down
**Hook Type:** Symptom to Root Cause

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DTI_VewD2Lk/ — @sampiliero
**Source structure breakdown:** Opens with a then-vs-now contrast on how Meta serves ads (audience-first → concept-first under Andromeda). Body walks an ad-library teardown of one big brand, pulling out three distinct creative pillars (templated product splash, product-in-use, raw UGC) and argues each reaches an audience the others can't. Closes on the reframe that "tiny iterations are dead, pillar concepts are not" — stacking pillars compounds reach and ROAS.

**On-screen hook text:** ONE WINNER ISN'T A STRATEGY
**Spoken hook:** "Your ad account stalls because every creative is chasing the same buyer."
**Hold line:** "You found one winner, copied it ten ways, and growth flatlined."

**Full script:**
"Your ad account stalls because every creative is chasing the same buyer. You found one winner, copied it ten ways, and growth flatlined. Meta doesn't reward more of the same ad. It rewards different concepts that reach buyers your winner never could. So stop hunting for the one ad. Build three or four real creative concepts, then make versions inside each. A clean product shot, a product-in-use shot, a raw customer video — each one opens an audience the others can't touch. Pillars scale. Copies plateau. That's the whole game now."

**Visual pattern interrupts (first 10s):**
- 0–2s: Tight punch-in, top overlay "ONE WINNER ISN'T A STRATEGY"
- 2–5s: Top-zone graphic: a single ad multiplying into 10 identical copies, then a flat line
- 5–10s: Top-zone diagram: 3 distinct pillar tiles → 3 separate audience circles

**Why this hook should perform:**
It names a symptom every advertiser has lived — scale stops even though the ad "works" — and immediately points at the real cause. No jargon, clear consequence (flatlined growth), and a curiosity gap that only resolves once you explain pillars. Filters for people actually running spend, not template hunters.

### Script 2: Your landing page is burning spend

**Topic:** The page behind your ad, not the ad itself, is what's spiking cost per lead.
**Pillar:** Hard Truth
**Hook Type:** Expensive Mistake

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DMwKogUv4dJ/ — @growzutrue
**Source structure breakdown:** Frames a "$10K vs $1M landing page" comparison. Body tears apart several weak pages sent traffic from Google — no headline, no above-the-fold form, not mobile-optimized, just a hero image — and ties each flaw to wasted cost-per-click and a $250 cost per lead. Closes on the "million-dollar" page: matching headline, interactive form up top, real pricing, testimonials, mobile-clean — dropping CPL to $20–30.

**On-screen hook text:** YOUR LANDING PAGE IS BURNING SPEND
**Spoken hook:** "You're paying Google for clicks and sending them to a page that can't convert."
**Hold line:** "No headline, no form near the top — just a pretty image they scroll past."

**Full script:**
"You're paying Google for clicks and sending them to a page that can't convert. No headline, no form near the top — just a pretty image they scroll past. That's how a lead goes from thirty bucks to two hundred and fifty. People give you four seconds. If the offer and the form aren't above the fold, they bounce back to the search results you just paid for. I'd fix the page before I touch a single bid. A headline that matches the ad, a one-thumb form, real pricing, real proof. The ad isn't your problem. The page eating the click is."

**Visual pattern interrupts (first 10s):**
- 0–2s: Punch-in, top overlay "$30 → $250 PER LEAD"
- 2–5s: Top-zone: mock landing page with a big red X over an empty hero image
- 5–10s: Top-zone before/after: "image only" vs "headline + form above the fold" with green check

**Why this hook should perform:**
It reframes a money problem advertisers blame on "the ads" and puts it on the page — an Expensive Mistake they didn't know they were making. The dollar jump ($30→$250) is concrete and stings. High-intent because only people spending on traffic feel it.

### Script 3: Don't touch the winning ad set

**Topic:** Launching new creative into a proven ad set resets its learning and crashes performance.
**Pillar:** Structural Tear-Down
**Hook Type:** Boundary Hook

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DYnbHQAM2Dx/ — @sampiliero
**Source structure breakdown:** Hook is a flat warning: new creatives into existing ad sets crash campaigns. Body explains the operator's rule — every new ad goes into its own new ad set inside one prospecting CBO, with min/max spend controls so new ads get tested without starving — and claims it held across ~100 brands in a year. Closes with a comment-trigger CTA for a 24-page guide.

**On-screen hook text:** DON'T TOUCH THE WINNING AD SET
**Spoken hook:** "Stop dropping new creatives into the ad set that's already working."
**Hold line:** "Every time you do, you reset the learning that made it win."

**Full script:**
"Stop dropping new creatives into the ad set that's already working. Every time you do, you reset the learning that made it win, and the whole thing crashes. New ads go into a new ad set. Every single time. I run them inside one prospecting CBO and let Meta push budget where it performs. Want more control? Set a minimum spend so the new ads actually get tested instead of starved. I've watched this hold up across dozens of accounts. The winner stays untouched. The new stuff proves itself. That's how you test without torching what's already paying you."

**Visual pattern interrupts (first 10s):**
- 0–2s: Punch-in, top overlay "DON'T TOUCH THE WINNER"
- 2–5s: Top-zone: a green "winning" ad set bar dropping to red the moment a new ad icon lands on it
- 5–10s: Top-zone diagram: one CBO box → multiple separate ad-set lanes, new ad entering its own lane

**Why this hook should perform:**
A Boundary Hook works because it names a specific place you shouldn't touch and the cost of touching it. Advertisers have all watched a winner die after "just adding one more ad." It promises a clean rule, not a hack.

### Script 4: An ad account isn't a funnel

**Topic:** Expensive ads are usually a funnel problem — cold traffic hitting a buy-now ad with no warm-up.
**Pillar:** Future-Proofing
**Hook Type:** Contrarian Reframe

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DYpOK7uR1v1/ — @realchasechappell
**Source structure breakdown:** Opens by crediting a nine-figure brand's "playbook." Body lays out a three-layer funnel: top-of-funnel educational content (myths, swaps, breakdowns), middle-funnel trust stacking (founder content, testimonials, comparisons), bottom-funnel retention/LTV (bundles, subscriptions). Argues trust is pre-built before the website, so they can scale aggressively. Closes that winners build full-funnel ecosystems, not ad accounts.

**On-screen hook text:** AN AD ACCOUNT ISN'T A FUNNEL
**Spoken hook:** "You don't have an ads problem. You've got a funnel that ends at the first purchase."
**Hold line:** "So you throw cold traffic at a buy-now ad and wonder why it's expensive."

**Full script:**
"You don't have an ads problem. You've got a funnel that ends at the first purchase. So you throw cold traffic straight at a buy-now ad and wonder why it's so expensive. The brands scaling past eight figures run three layers. Top: content that teaches before it sells — myths, swaps, breakdowns. Middle: trust — founder content, testimonials, comparisons. Bottom: retention — bundles, subscriptions, repeat orders that lift lifetime value. By the time they pitch, the trust is already built. One ad sells once. A funnel compounds. Build the layers, not just the account."

**Visual pattern interrupts (first 10s):**
- 0–2s: Punch-in, top overlay "ADS ≠ FUNNEL"
- 2–5s: Top-zone: cold-traffic arrow slamming into a "BUY NOW" wall and bouncing off
- 5–10s: Top-zone stacked diagram: Teach → Trust → Retain layers building up

**Why this hook should perform:**
It flips the advertiser's framing — "my ads are too expensive" becomes "my funnel is missing layers." A Contrarian Reframe lands when it's familiar but reorganized. The cold-traffic image makes the gap obvious in three seconds.

### Script 5: Stop hiding a good product

**Topic:** Overproduced creative is often a cover for weak product positioning; simple ads win when the product earns it.
**Pillar:** Hard Truth
**Hook Type:** Founder Mirror

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DOdxntIjb9l/ — @realchasechappell
**Source structure breakdown:** Opens on a founder who scaled past $5M by "letting the product and lifestyle do the talking." Body contrasts overcomplicated ads with this brand's direct product shots and short copy — clothing, bags, the story, the making — and credits authenticity for organic celebrity wear. Closes that staying true to the product is how you build a multi-million-dollar brand, with a comment-trigger CTA.

**On-screen hook text:** STOP HIDING A GOOD PRODUCT
**Spoken hook:** "You're overproducing your ads to cover for a product you won't let speak."
**Hold line:** "You're paying a production tax to hide an offer that should sell itself."

**Full script:**
"You're overproducing your ads to cover for a product you won't let speak. You're paying a production tax — fancy edits, heavy effects, ten messages crammed into fifteen seconds — to hide an offer that should sell itself. Meanwhile the brands quietly scaling past seven figures run dead-simple creative. Direct product shots. Short copy. The story, the making, the actual thing. That's it. When the product's genuinely good, complicated ads just get in the way. So before you brief another big production, ask if you're dressing up the creative because the offer can't stand on its own. Simple converts when the product earns it. I'd strip it back before I'd spend more."

**Visual pattern interrupts (first 10s):**
- 0–2s: Punch-in, top overlay "STOP HIDING THE PRODUCT"
- 2–5s: Top-zone: an over-edited ad with stickers/effects getting stripped down to a clean product shot
- 5–10s: Top-zone checklist: "Product shot · short copy · the real story"

**Why this hook should perform:**
A Founder Mirror calls the viewer out personally — most operators secretly over-edit. It implies the uncomfortable truth (the offer can't stand alone) without insulting them, then offers a cheaper path. High-intent: only brand owners feel this.

### Script 6: Turn off Google's default settings

**Topic:** Google's default Display + Search Partners networks (and loose location/scheduling) quietly drain Search budgets.
**Pillar:** Structural Tear-Down
**Hook Type:** Impending Threat

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DXWc6NyiWVh/ — @growzutrue
**Source structure breakdown:** A step-by-step Google Ads build for service businesses. Body walks campaign creation and flags the key money-savers: turn OFF the Display Network and Search Partners ("they'll ruin your campaign"), set a tight location radius, schedule ads to business hours, and point traffic at a conversion-ready page (form up top, social proof, real photos, big call button, mobile-friendly). Closes deferring conversion tracking to a longer tutorial.

**On-screen hook text:** TURN OFF GOOGLE'S DEFAULTS
**Spoken hook:** "Google flips on two settings by default that quietly drain your budget."
**Hold line:** "And almost nobody finds them until they've already burned the first few hundred bucks."

**Full script:**
"Google flips on two settings by default that quietly drain your budget. And almost nobody finds them until they've already burned the first few hundred bucks. They're the Display Network and Search Partners — both on the second you build a Search campaign, both spending where your buyers aren't. Turn them off. Then tighten your location to where you actually serve, not the whole country. Schedule ads to your business hours so you're not paying for 3am clicks nobody answers. And send them to a page with a form up top and real reviews. Most wasted Google spend isn't your keywords. It's the defaults you never switched off."

**Visual pattern interrupts (first 10s):**
- 0–2s: Punch-in, top overlay "GOOGLE'S DEFAULTS ARE COSTING YOU"
- 2–5s: Top-zone: two toggle switches glowing ON (red), labeled only "DEFAULT: ON" — names hidden (hold the reveal)
- 5–10s: Top-zone: the toggles flip OFF (green) and reveal their labels — "Display Network" + "Search Partners"

**Why this hook should perform:**
An Impending Threat hook works because the danger is already live in their account, not hypothetical. "On by default" makes it feel like the platform did this to them. It's the run's clearest Google-Ads angle and pulls service-business owners specifically.

### Script 7: 96% of your ads will lose

**Topic:** The small share of ads that win share three traits — and none are budget or targeting.
**Pillar:** Structural Tear-Down
**Hook Type:** Specific Blueprint

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DYOBtsSJTqN/ — @growzutrue
**Source structure breakdown:** Hook states that of 1,500 ads tested, only 4% (60) won. Body breaks the three shared traits: a real person on camera (not a polished actor), 30–60s length (room to explain problem/fix/trust), and a strong first-three-seconds hook (curiosity, contradiction, or open-loop — not "check this out"). Names an "ugly ad formula" sequence and closes with a comment-trigger for the blueprint.

**On-screen hook text:** 96% OF YOUR ADS WILL LOSE
**Spoken hook:** "Ninety-six percent of the ads you test will lose. The winners share three things."
**Hold line:** "And none of them are budget or targeting."

**Full script:**
"Ninety-six percent of the ads you test will lose. The winners share three things, and none are budget or targeting. One: a real person on camera. Not a polished actor — a founder or customer explaining something like they mean it. Two: length. Thirty to sixty seconds, enough to name the problem, show the fix, build trust. Ten-second clips don't convert unless the page finishes the job. Three: the first three seconds. Open a loop or challenge a belief — don't say 'check this out.' Real face, enough room, sharp hook. That's the pattern under almost every winner."

**Visual pattern interrupts (first 10s):**
- 0–2s: Punch-in, top overlay "96% LOSE"
- 2–5s: Top-zone: a grid of 100 ad tiles, 96 fading gray, 4 lighting up green
- 5–10s: Top-zone numbered list building: "1 Real person · 2 30–60s · 3 First 3 seconds"

**Why this hook should perform:**
A Specific Blueprint hook trades agitation for proof — the 96/4 stat is concrete and the three traits are immediately usable. "None are budget or targeting" kills the two excuses advertisers reach for first, forcing them to keep watching for the real answer.

### Script 8: One ad can't do five jobs

**Pillar:** Hard Truth
**Hook Type:** Symptom to Root Cause

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DYmpPirRiPS/ — @realchasechappell
**Source structure breakdown:** Rapid teardown of several brands, each credited with mastering ONE creative lever: problem/solution clarity, demo-driven proof, founder-led authenticity, instantly recognizable branding, entertainment, or creator volume. The throughline is that each brand's creative communicates a single idea in three seconds. Closes with a comment-trigger for a creative playbook.

**On-screen hook text:** ONE AD CAN'T DO FIVE JOBS
**Spoken hook:** "Your ads look clean and still lose because each one's doing five jobs at once."
**Hold line:** "So the algorithm can't tell who it's for — and neither can the buyer."

**Full script:**
"Your ads look clean and still lose because each one's trying to do five jobs at once. So the algorithm can't tell who it's for, and neither can the buyer. Fit, price, proof, brand, story — all crammed into fifteen seconds. The brands that actually scale pick one job per creative and nail it. One ad is pure problem-and-solution. Another is a demo that proves itself on camera. Another is raw founder talk that builds trust. Another is just unmistakable branding. Each creative carries a single idea a viewer gets in three seconds. Stop building one ad that says everything. Build several that each say one thing perfectly."

**Visual pattern interrupts (first 10s):**
- 0–2s: Punch-in, top overlay "ONE AD, FIVE JOBS = LOSS"
- 2–5s: Top-zone: a single ad tile overloaded with 5 labels, glitching
- 5–10s: Top-zone: that tile splitting into 4 clean tiles, each with one label

**Why this hook should perform:**
Symptom ("looks clean, still loses") to root cause ("five jobs at once") is the strongest default for creative problems. It validates that their ads aren't ugly — they're overloaded — which is easier to accept and act on. Clear, original, and free of jargon.

### Script 10: Iterations aren't dead

**Topic:** "Iterations are dead" is misread — tiny tweaks are dead, but real versions of a proven concept are how you scale.
**Pillar:** Future-Proofing
**Hook Type:** Contrarian Reframe

**Source video:** https://www.instagram.com/p/DTI_VewD2Lk/ — @sampiliero *(reuse of the top-ranked source, different angle from Script 1)*
**Source structure breakdown:** Same teardown as Script 1. The angle here pulls from the closing argument: the creator distinguishes "tiny iterations are dead" from building real versions of a winning concept, which he calls the actual path to incremental audiences and scale. Script 1 borrowed the pillar-discovery beat; this one borrows the iteration-reframe beat.

**On-screen hook text:** ITERATIONS AREN'T DEAD
**Spoken hook:** "Somebody told you iterations are dead, so you killed your best concept too early."
**Hold line:** "Tiny tweaks are dead. Real versions of a proven concept are how you scale."

**Full script:**
"Somebody told you iterations are dead, so you killed your best concept too early. Let's separate two things. Changing a button color and calling it a new ad — that's dead, that does nothing. But taking a concept that's already converting and building ten real versions of it? That's not dead. That's the entire job. A proven concept is a vein. You mine it — new angles, new hooks, new openers on the same winning idea. Most accounts don't fail from too many iterations. They fail because the owner abandoned a winner chasing the next shiny thing."

**Visual pattern interrupts (first 10s):**
- 0–2s: Punch-in, top overlay "ITERATIONS AREN'T DEAD"
- 2–5s: Top-zone: "button color change" tile with a red X vs "same concept, 10 angles" tiles with a green check
- 5–10s: Top-zone: a single winning concept branching into multiple version tiles

**Why this hook should perform:**
A Contrarian Reframe against a piece of advice the audience has heard and half-believed. It resolves a real confusion (what "iteration" even means) and reframes abandoning winners as the actual mistake — which protects their best-performing work. Pairs as a sharper bookend to Script 1.
